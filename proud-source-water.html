About the Brand

Proud Source Water is a U.S.-raised bottled water company that offers naturally alkaline spring water, sustainably sourced and packaged in infinitely recyclable aluminum bottles.

My Deliverable
  • Brand Strategy & Positioning
  • Slogan Development
  • Logo & Logotype Refinement
  • Can Packaging Design
  • Photography Art Direction
  • Marketing Campaign Direction
01

The Challenge

Proud Source wanted to reposition itself in the market, establish a new target audience, and build a marketing strategy and brand identity that would appeal to that audience.

Proud Source started as a small startup from Mackay, Idaho, with a mission to create jobs in a small mountain town. They quickly gained traction across the U.S., with their focus on sustainable practices and their premium, yet accessible product. In just a few years, this small-town brand became a main staple on shelves across the country. The only problem was that the scope of their messaging was too narrow to speak to the new customers they were now in front of.

That's where I came in. I was brought in to redirect this message and build a brand that would appeal to a wider audience.

The brand slogan was "Make Ours Different", and their imagery showcased rural, mountain, and outdoor settings. This imagery is familiar to some, but someone in the heart of Detroit probably doesn't see themselves reflected in that imagery.

Also, this strategy of describing your product and telling people how good it is puts the brand in a position of having to ask customers to buy into who they are — which is a weaker place to sell from. So I needed to reframe that message.

02

The Approach

I created a clean-looking design that allows their product to fit in any setting and a brand message that pulls from them.

We started by going back to the name of the brand and thinking about what "Proud Source" means. The name is a statement of trust between the product and the desire to share it. We came up with the slogan "Proud of This". Obviously that slogan is saying "we are proud of our product", but it takes the brand an action step further — and that line works for anybody. It doesn't mean you need to be the type of person who makes up Proud Source's customer base. They don't have to abandon the original audience or storytelling about the product, but they can expand their voice to speak to new potential customers.

It's also not asking the customer to buy into what the brand is. Instead, it's reflecting an emotion back to them so they already share the customer's sentiment. The customer no longer has to decide "is this something that fits into my life?" because the messaging communicates that connection already.

With that direction in mind, I started expanding on the original brand identity. Their original water droplet logo wasn't very original — multiple other brands operate in the same space with this exact same concept. The logotype without the water droplet was already interesting and cool, but was being underutilized. So I removed the droplet and let the typography stand on its own.

To lean into that American culture messaging, I decided to reduce the emphasis on graphic elements in photo treatments and instead do the opposite — showcase a diverse range of American cultures where their customers could see themselves represented. Fewer graphic overlays, more lifestyle photography. In this way, we could tell a visual story of the brand's expanded voice: let the marketing assets do the heavy lifting, and let the brand design accentuate that story.

I wanted the can to be noticeable in photography without being loud — something simple and bold that fits in any setting. The bright color block became the key visual element, whether the can is on a table, clipped to a backpack on a mountain, or being grabbed out of a cooler in the middle of New York.

03

The Outcome

I created a brand that speaks directly to their target audiences and remains focused on their mission of delivering the best quality water.

The work did what repositioning is supposed to do — it gave the brand a bigger stage without asking it to abandon where it came from. The Idaho roots, the sustainability mission, the premium product — none of that changed. What changed was who felt invited into that story. By shifting the message from describing the product to reflecting the customer's own pride back at them, Proud Source now had something to say to someone in Detroit just as naturally as someone in the Rockies.

The visual identity followed the same logic. Removing the water droplet and letting the typography carry the weight gave the brand a cleaner, more confident presence on shelf. The color block gave the can an anchor — something that reads immediately whether it's at a farmers market, a rooftop bar, or the back of a pickup truck. The brand got more flexible without getting less specific.
